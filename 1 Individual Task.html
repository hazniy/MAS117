The maths behind it

  • The first rook can be placed on any one of the $64$ squares of the board. Hence, the second rook may fall on one of the remaining $63$ squares, because it cannot fall in a position of the already placed first rook (Figure 1). Hence the total ways of placing two rooks on the board is $64.63 = 4032$ This number will include challenging and non-challenging configurations.

  • The Non challenging configurations would be when the two rooks are in the different rows and different columns. To count these we use the following reasoning

    1. The first can go on any of the $64$ squares.

    2. For the second rook to be at a non-attacking position, it must be in a different row and a different column. Since one row and one column is already occupied by the first rook, there are $7$ choices for the row and $7$ choices for the column for the second rook. Thus, the number of non-attacking configurations is $64.7.7 = 3136$

  • To find the number of challenging configurations, we simply take the total number of configurations and subtract the non challenging ones, $4032 - 3136 = 896$.

  • The probability, within the simulation context, that a random placement is non-challenging is $P(non-challenging) = \frac{Number of non-challenging configurations}{Total number of configurations} = \frac{3136}{4032} = 0.78$. That would suggest that about $78%$ of random placements of two rooks are non-challenging. P/S It isn't an exact $78%$ non-challenging configurations are to be derived every time (Figure 3). This has to do with the number of placements that were allowed for the rooks in each simulation and how 'random' the selections have been made. It is, however, constrained by simple mathematics-the ratio of the number of non-challenging configurations versus the total possible-to never rise above $78%$. The number of non-challenging configurations relates to the total possible, and thus it can never go over a ratio value of about $78%$.

  • Well, the Law of Large Numbers says that the more trials you do, the closer the average of the results will get to the expected value. For the simulation, this means the more simulations run, the closer the proportion of non-challenging configurations will be to the theoretical probability of $0.78$.

Maths explanation

This allows combinatorics to provide us the count of the number of non-challenging configurations to put three rooks that no two of them are located in the same row or in the same column (Figure 4). Since first one has to choose $3$ rows out of the total of $8$ available that can be done in $\binom{3}{8} = 56$ ways. Similarly, we have to choose $3$ columns out of the $8$, and this also can be done in $\binom{8}{3}=56$ ways.Once the rows and columns are chosen, in the selected rows and columns, the three rooks can be arranged in $3! = 6$ ways, because the first rook has $3$ column options, the second rook has $2$, and the third has $1$. The total number of non-challenging configurations is then $\binom{8}{3}.\binom{8}{3}.3! = 56.56.6 = 18 816$. We now have to find the probability of the non-attacking configuration. Let us first get the number of ways one can place $3$ rooks at random on the board. That would be the number of ways of choosing $3$ squares out of the $64$ squares of the chessboard which is $\binom{64}{3} = 41 664$.Then the probability of a non-attacking configuration is given by the ratio of non-attacking configurations to the total configurations, that is, $P(non-challenging) = \frac{18 816}{41 664} = 0.45$.Thus, there is a $45%$ chance that a random placement of 3 rooks on the chessboard will result in a non-attacking configuration (Figure 5).
