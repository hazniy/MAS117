Knight's Tour

Definitions

One of the most famous mathematical problems debated concerning whether any such sequence of moves is plausible is the Knight's Tour problem. In a sense, this problem provides an immediate application of the Knight's Tour, where the knight must step in turn on successive knight moves without stepping onto the same square again. A complete tour means stepping on all squares once, whereas a closed complete tour would mean ending on a square sufficiently near to this configuration for the knight to return to the starting square.

How to determine the correct next move of the knight?

  • Graph theory

    Graph theory gives us insight into the knight's movement across a chessboard. Each of the squares is thought of as the nodes in a graph, and each legal knight move corresponds to an edge between two nodes. The knight's movement proceeds in "L" shapes: two squares in one direction and one square in a direction perpendicular. Because of this, from any square in the middle of the board, barring the edges or corners, there could be up to eight different possible moves, while fewer than eight available moves are possible from some squares.

  • Hamiltonian path

    In the glossary of graph theory, such a path is referred to as a Hamiltonian path. If a knight can return to its original square after traversing every square, it will form a Hamiltonian cycle. This is notoriously difficult, it is impractical to evaluate even a meaningful subset of paths as the size of the board grows and the number of paths increases massively really fast.

  • Challenges of Hamiltonian cycle

    The main difficulty in this case arises from the rather imbalanced connectivities of those nodes. Some squares especially those near the center of the board offer way more move options than those near the edges. This imbalance makes the consideration of finding an acceptable path likely to visit every square interfere with the knight's movement. One of the common heuristics used to assist the knight in completing the Hamiltonian path would be Warnsdorff's Rule.

  • Warnsdorff's Rule

    Warnsdorff's Rule relates to the heuristic method for arriving at the solution more effectively by shortening the possibilities at any step of the Knight's tour problem. It works based on a simple and very efficient idea: the knight goes to the square from which it has the fewest onward moves. In this way, the knight avoids being trapped in the parts of the board that do not allow access to most, if all, of the squares.

Maths Explanation

An $n.n$ chessboard will have $n^2$ squares, this can also be counted to verify. If a knight happens to start on any square, the next thing to determine is the number of ways of visiting the remaining $n^2 -1$ squares, which is given by the permutation formula $P = (n^2 -1)!$. There would be a total of $8.8=64$ squares on the standard chessboard, and therefore after starting, the number of permutations on the remaining $63$ squares by the knight would equal $P = 63! = 4.33.10^{86}$ a huge number indeed. Thus this stands impractical for calculation for larger chessboards.

If a knight is placed on a central square, then it can make up to 8 moves, on approaching edges, the possibilities start winding down, and finally corner moves provide you with fewer options. Therefore counting the allowed moves to be determined from any square by means of a position $C = \binom{8}{n}$ where n is the number of legal moves afforded at the position of the knight. $C =\binom{8}{8}= 1$, if C is on Central. $C =\binom{8}{4}= 70$, if C is on edge.
